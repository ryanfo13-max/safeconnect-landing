Common Questions We Hear From Fleet Operators

Facilities need three-phase power delivering 480V for optimal eTRU efficiency — though 230V infrastructure can work, it operates at lower efficiency. Kilowatt requirements vary by TRU model, and your power company can assess whether your existing capacity is sufficient. Some electricity providers will even fund infrastructure upgrades to deliver the additional capacity needed.
Yes — most existing facilities can be upgraded. The key steps are establishing access to 3-phase power at dock doors and trailer staging areas, distributing electrical circuitry to parking lots and staging areas, and adding protective equipment to prevent accidental contact with live connections. SafeConnect's double-dock pedestal designs can also reduce infrastructure costs significantly during the upgrade process.
The biggest ROI drivers are fuel savings (electricity costs are significantly lower and far more predictable than diesel), maintenance reduction (electric motors cost considerably less to maintain than idling diesel engines), and extended equipment life — some companies report a 30% increase in TRU run life after switching. Most companies also significantly underestimate how often TRU idling occurs, meaning real-world fuel savings typically exceed initial projections.
Timelines vary based on your facility's existing electrical infrastructure and utility coordination requirements. The process covers six key steps: assessing power availability, planning capacity, designing electrical distribution, installing infrastructure, preparing trailers, and training staff. Projects move fastest when power availability and facility design are addressed early — utility lead times are typically the longest variable.
The most common mistakes are: overlooking electrical capacity requirements before purchasing equipment, underestimating how often TRU idling actually occurs, neglecting circuitry design optimization (and missing double-station cost savings), improper mounting of equipment creating electrocution hazards, inadequate protection of live connections from backing vehicles, and failing to prepare trailer infrastructure as part of the conversion plan.
